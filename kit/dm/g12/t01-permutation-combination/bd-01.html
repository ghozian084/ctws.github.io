Meeting 1: The Factorial Function (Part 1)

Main Concept Factorials ($n!$)

  • Definition: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \dots \times 1$
  • Used to find the total number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects in a row.
  • Special Case: $0! = 1$
  • Property: $n! = n \times (n-1)!$

Activity The Slot Method Discovery

Hook: "Imagine organizing an Indonesian Independence Day ceremony. You have 4 different flags to arrange in a row on stage. How many possible visual arrangements are there?"

Task: Draw 4 empty "slots". Fill in how many choices you have for the first slot, the second, etc. Multiply them together!

Meeting 1: The Factorial Function (Part 2)

Sample Question Evaluating Expressions

Evaluate the following without expanding every single number:

  1. $\frac{8!}{6!}$
  2. $\frac{10!}{8!2!}$

Exit Ticket

Simplify this algebraic expression completely: $\frac{n!}{(n-2)!}$

Meeting 2: Permutations (Part 1)

Main Concept Permutations ($^nP_r$)

  • Rule: ORDER MATTERS! (Arrangements, passwords, positions).
  • Formula: ${}^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$
  • Choosing $r$ objects from $n$ distinct objects and arranging them in a line.

Activity The OSIS Election

Hook: "From 10 top students, SMA Semesta needs to elect an OSIS President, Vice President, and Secretary."

Task: Is Andi as Pres and Budi as VP the same as Budi as Pres and Andi as VP? No! Use the slot method first ($10 \times 9 \times 8$), then try to derive the factorial formula from it.

Sample Question Evaluating $^nP_r$

In how many ways can 5 people be seated in a row of 7 chairs?

Hint: Think of it as arranging 5 people out of 7 available positions.

Exit Ticket

5 boys and 4 girls sit in a row. How many arrangements are there if all the girls MUST sit together? (Treat the girls as one 'block').

Meeting 3: Identical Objects & Restrictions (Part 1)

Main Concept Dividing out Duplicates

  • When objects are identical, swapping them doesn't create a new arrangement. We are overcounting!
  • Formula: $\frac{n!}{p!q!r!}$ where $p, q, r$ are the frequencies of the identical items.

Activity The Repeated Letter Problem

Hook: "BATIK has 5 different letters ($5!$ ways). What about the word SEMESTA?"

Task: Experiment with the word "MAM". Write out all actual visual arrangements. How does it compare to $3!$? Derive why we must divide by $2!$.

Meeting 4: Combinations (Part 1)

Main Concept Combinations ($^nC_r$)

  • Rule: ORDER DOES NOT MATTER! (Selecting teams, groups, committees).
  • Formula: ${}^nC_r = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$
  • Symmetry: ${}^{10}C_2 = {}^{10}C_8$. Choosing 2 to go is the same as choosing 8 to stay!

Activity Removing the Order

Hook: "We need a team of 3 for a National Debate in Jakarta. Is a team of Andi, Budi, Citra different from Budi, Citra, Andi?"

Task: Start with the permutation $\frac{10!}{7!}$. Since 3 people can be arranged in $3!$ ways amongst themselves, how do we remove these duplicates? (Divide by $3!$).

Meeting 5: Problem Solving (AND/OR) (Part 1)

Main Concept Counting Principles

  • The word AND implies Multiplication ($\times$).
  • The word OR implies Addition ($+$).
  • "At least 1" Shortcut:
    (Total possible ways) $-$ (Ways with ZERO of that item).

Activity The AND/OR Dilemma

Hook: "An Indonesian cultural committee of 4 is to be chosen from 5 boys and 6 girls."

Task: Debate how to calculate:
1) Choose 2 boys AND 2 girls.
2) Choose 4 boys OR 4 girls.

Meeting 5: Problem Solving (AND/OR) (Part 2)

Exit Ticket

Meeting 6: Chapter Review (Part 1)

Main Concept The Decision Tree

  • Does Order Matter?
    $\rightarrow$ YES: Permutation ($P$) or Slot Method.
    $\rightarrow$ NO: Combination ($C$).
  • Are there identical items?
    $\rightarrow$ YES: Divide by $p!q!$

Activity Mind Mapping

Hook: "Exams mix P and C together. You must know how to choose the right weapon."

Task: In groups, draw a massive decision tree on A3 paper connecting P, C, Identical objects, AND/OR rules, and factorial notation.

Meeting 6: Evaluation (Part 2)

Sample Question Identify P or C

State whether the following require Permutation or Combination:

  1. A 4-digit PIN code.
  2. Picking 3 fruits from a bowl of 10.
  3. Seating 5 guests around a table.

Exit Ticket

Self-Reflection: Which type of problem (Permutation with restriction or Combinations with 'at least') do you need more practice on?
