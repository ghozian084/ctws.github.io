Lesson Plan: Permutations and Combinations

School: SMA Semesta 2026/2025 | Grade: 12

Time Allocation: 6 Meetings (2 x 40 minutes = 80 mins per meeting)

📌 Topic: 5. Permutations and combinations

Reference: Cambridge International AS & A Level Math: Prob & Stat 1

Method: 5E & Problem Based Learning (PBL)

Approach: Deep Learning & Realistic Math Education (RME)

Character Focus: Self-Regulated, Collaboration, Citizenship, Creativity

Differentiation: Extension, Scaffolding, EAL, SEN

🎯 Objectives

  • 5.2.1: Understand and use the notation $n!$ (factorial) including evaluating numerical expressions.
  • 5.2.2: Understand and use permutations in simple cases including evaluating numerical expressions.
  • 5.2.3: Understand and use combinations in simple cases including evaluating numerical expressions and use the relationship between combinations and binomial coefficients.
  • 5.2.4: Solve problems using permutations and combinations including problems involving selections and arrangements.

📅 Meeting 1: The Factorial Function (Subtopic 5.1)

E1 ENGAGE | PBL Problem Introduction [15 minutes]
Contextual Hook
(Teacher-led)

🪝 Teacher Hook — Slide 1 (10 min):

  • Citizenship Context: "Imagine you are organizing an Indonesian Independence Day ceremony. You have 4 different flags to arrange in a row on stage. How many possible visual arrangements are there?"

Problem Statement (5 min):

  • "If we had 10 flags, counting them manually would take years. How do we model this mathematically?"
E2 EXPLORE | Discovering the Multiplication Principle [20 minutes]
Collaborative Group Work
(Student-led)

Slot Method Discovery (20 min):

  • Groups draw 4 empty "slots" representing flagpoles.
  • Meaningful: Students deduce: "For the first slot, I have 4 choices. For the second, only 3 choices left..." $\rightarrow 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1$.
  • Calculate the answer ($24$).
  • Joyful: Groups race to find the number of ways to arrange 5 and then 6 objects.
E3 EXPLAIN | Defining Factorial [20 minutes]
Direct Instruction
(Teacher-led)

Direct Instruction — Slides 2–4 (20 min):

  • Slide 2: Introduce notation: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \dots \times 1$.
  • Slide 3: Algebraic properties: Show that $n! = n \times (n-1)!$.
  • Slide 4: The special case: Define $0! = 1$ and briefly explain why (mathematical consistency).
  • Mindful Check: "Is $5! + 2!$ equal to $7!$?" (No! Prove it algebraically on the whiteboard).
Scaffold students: EAL students ensure they pronounce "!" as "factorial", not an exclamation mark of surprise.
E4 & E5 | Evaluating Expressions & Exit Slip [25 minutes]
Independent Practice
& Teacher Check

Elaborate (15 min):

  • Students evaluate expressions by canceling out terms: $\frac{8!}{6!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6!}{6!} = 56$.

🎟️ Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • Simplify algebraically: $\frac{n!}{(n-2)!}$. (Expected answer: $n^2 - n$).

📅 Meeting 2: Permutations (Subtopic 5.2)

E1 ENGAGE | OSIS Election Hook [10 minutes]
Interactive Q&A
(Teacher-led)

🪝 Teacher Hook — Slide 1 (10 min):

  • Citizenship Context: "From 10 top students, SMA Semesta needs to elect an OSIS President, Vice President, and Secretary."
  • Ask: "If Andi is President and Budi is VP, is that the same arrangement as Budi as President and Andi as VP?"
  • Meaningful: Establish that ORDER MATTERS. This is a Permutation.
E2 EXPLORE | Slot Method to Formula [20 minutes]
Pair Work
(Student-led)

Deriving the Formula (20 min):

  • Students use the Slot Method: $10 \times 9 \times 8 = 720$.
  • Teacher challenges pairs: "How can we write $10 \times 9 \times 8$ using factorials?"
  • Hint provided: Multiply by $\frac{7!}{7!}$. Students discover $\frac{10!}{7!} = \frac{10!}{(10-3)!}$.
E3 EXPLAIN | Permutation Formula [20 minutes]
Direct Instruction
(Teacher-led)

Direct Instruction — Slides 2–4 (20 min):

  • Slide 2: Formalize the formula: ${}^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$.
  • Slide 3: Explain calculator usage for ${}^nP_r$.
  • Slide 4: Conditional permutations (e.g., "Two specific people MUST sit together"). Model treating the two people as one "block".
Scaffold students: Low achievers are encouraged to ALWAYS draw the 'Slot Method' boxes first before attempting to plug numbers into the formula.
E4 & E5 | Application & Evaluation [30 minutes]
Independent Task
& Exit Slip

Elaborate (20 min):

  • Students complete Worksheet 2 (Part A) on permutations without identical objects.

🎟️ Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • In how many ways can 5 people be seated in a row of 7 chairs? Evaluate using the ${}^nP_r$ formula.

📅 Meeting 3: Permutations with Restrictions / Identical Objects (Subtopic 5.2/5.4)

E1 & E2 | The Repeated Letter Problem [20 minutes]
PBL Hook &
Exploration

🪝 Teacher Hook — Slide 1 (5 min):

  • Write the word "BATIK" on the board. (5 letters, all different = $5!$ ways).
  • Write the word "SEMESTA". "Are there still $7!$ ways? What happens when I swap the two 'S's?"

Exploration (15 min):

  • Students experiment with a smaller word: "MAM". (M1-A-M2 vs M2-A-M1 look identical).
  • Realization: We are overcounting! We must divide out the duplicates.
E3 EXPLAIN | Identical Objects Formula [20 minutes]
Direct Instruction
(Teacher-led)

Direct Instruction — Slides 2–4 (20 min):

  • Slide 2: Formula: $\frac{n!}{p!q!r!}$ for identical objects.
  • Slide 3: Example: PANCASILA (9 letters, 3 A's). Ways = $\frac{9!}{3!}$.
  • Slide 4: Mindful Check: "What if all letters are the same? e.g., 'AAAA'. $\frac{4!}{4!} = 1$ way. Does this make logical sense?" (Yes).
Scaffold students: Extension students calculate arrangements of "INDONESIA" where all vowels MUST be together.
E4 & E5 | Independent Practice & Exit [40 minutes]
Independent Task
& Exit Slip

Elaborate (30 min):

  • Students complete Worksheet 2 (Part B). Includes complex restrictions (e.g., arrangements of "STATISTICS" starting and ending with 'S').
  • Self-Regulated: Students verify their logic by writing out the specific condition fraction (e.g., fix the ends, calculate the middle).

🎟️ Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • Calculate the number of different arrangements of the word "MATHEMATICS".

📅 Meeting 4: Combinations (Subtopic 5.3)

E1 ENGAGE | Combination vs Permutation Hook [15 minutes]
Class Debate
(Teacher-led)

🪝 Teacher Hook — Slide 1 (15 min):

  • "We need a team of 3 students to represent the school in a National Debate in Jakarta. From 10 students, we pick Andi, Budi, and Citra."
  • "If I picked Budi, Citra, and Andi, is it a different team?"
  • Meaningful: Establish that ORDER DOES NOT MATTER. This is a Combination.
E2 EXPLORE | Removing the Order [15 minutes]
Collaborative
(Student-led)

Logic Discovery (15 min):

  • If picking 3 people from 10 where order matters is $\frac{10!}{7!}$.
  • But for any 3 people (A, B, C), they can be arranged in $3!$ ways.
  • How do we remove these $3!$ extra arrangements? Divide!
  • Students derive $\frac{10!}{3!7!}$.
E3 EXPLAIN | Combination Formula & Binomials [20 minutes]
Direct Instruction
(Teacher-led)

Direct Instruction — Slides 2–4 (20 min):

  • Slide 2: Formalize the formula: ${}^nC_r = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$.
  • Slide 3: Symmetry property: Why is ${}^{10}C_2 = {}^{10}C_8$? (Choosing 2 to go is the same as choosing 8 to stay behind).
  • Slide 4: Briefly link ${}^nC_r$ to Pascal's Triangle and Binomial coefficients.
Scaffold students: Write keywords explicitly on the board. PERMUTATION = Arrange, Line up, Code, Password. COMBINATION = Select, Choose, Group, Team.
E4 & E5 | Application & Evaluation [30 minutes]
Independent Task

Elaborate (20 min):

  • Worksheet 3. Basic combinations and identifying whether a scenario is P or C.

🎟️ Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • A pizza parlor offers 8 toppings. How many different 3-topping pizzas can be ordered?

📅 Meeting 5: Problem Solving with Permutations & Combinations (Subtopic 5.4)

E1 & E2 | The "AND" vs "OR" Dilemma [20 minutes]
Teacher-led &
PBL Work

🪝 Hook & Explore — Slide 1 (20 min):

  • Culturally STEM: "An Indonesian cultural committee of 4 is to be chosen from 5 boys and 6 girls."
  • Problem 1: Choose 2 boys AND 2 girls.
  • Problem 2: Choose 4 boys OR 4 girls.
  • Collaboration: Groups debate whether to multiply or add the combinations.
E3 EXPLAIN | Fundamental Counting Principles [15 minutes]
Direct Instruction

Direct Instruction — Slides 2-3 (15 min):

  • Slide 2: Establish the golden rule: AND = Multiply ($\times$). OR = Add ($+$).
  • Slide 3: Solving "At least" problems. (e.g., "At least 1 girl" = (1G AND 3B) + (2G AND 2B) + (3G AND 1B) + (4G AND 0B)).
  • Mindful: Teach the shortcut for "At least 1": (Total possible ways) $-$ (Ways with ZERO girls).
E4 & E5 | Complex Problem Solving [45 minutes]
Independent Task
& Exit Slip

Elaborate (35 min):

  • Distribute Cambridge past paper questions (Worksheet 4) mixing permutations and combinations.
  • Students must first highlight the "Trigger Words" (select vs arrange) before solving.

🎟️ Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • A team of 5 is chosen from 4 doctors and 6 nurses. Find the number of ways if the team must contain exactly 2 doctors.
Scaffold students: Extension students tackle problems combining both: "Select a team of 4 from 10, then arrange them in a line for a photo." (${}^{10}C_4 \times 4!$).

📅 Meeting 6: Chapter Review & Summative Evaluation

E1 & E2 | Collaborative Review (Mind Mapping) [20 minutes]
Student-led Review

🪝 Concept Mapping (20 min):

  • Groups use A3 paper to map out a "Decision Tree" to use during exams.
  • Example node: "Does order matter?" $\rightarrow$ Yes (Permutation) / No (Combination).
  • "Are there repeating objects?" $\rightarrow$ Yes (Divide by $p!q!$).
E4 & E5 | Chapter Evaluation & Closure [60 minutes]
Summative Test
(Teacher-monitored)

Chapter 5 Test (50 min):

  • Remove visual aids. Distribute the Chapter 5 Evaluation.
  • Self-Regulated: Students pace themselves under strict exam conditions, ensuring they read "select" vs "arrange" carefully.

Closure & Exit Ticket (10 min):

  • Self-Reflection: "Which type of problem (Permutation with restriction or Combinations with 'at least') do you need more practice on for the AS Level exams?"
🌟 Differentiation Strategies
Profile Strategies & Actions in Class
Extension (High Achievers) Provide circular permutation problems (e.g., seating around a round table: $(n-1)!$). Challenge them to solve "at least 1" problems using the complement method (Total - None) and algebraically prove why ${}^nC_r = {}^nC_{n-r}$.
Scaffolding (Middle/Low) Mandate the use of the "Slot Method" for all permutation problems before using the formula. Provide a visual flowchart: "Does Order Matter? -> Yes (P) / No (C)" on their desks.
EAL (English Additional Language) Provide a glossary mapping English trigger words to math operators: And (Dan) = Multiply, Or (Atau) = Add, Arrange/Line up (Susun) = Permutation, Select/Team (Pilih) = Combination.
SEN (Special Educational Needs) Use physical manipulatives (different colored blocks) for early permutation exercises. Break down multi-part word problems into explicit bullet-pointed steps.

📝 Worksheets Compilation (For Practice & Homework)

Worksheet 1 & 2: Factorials and Permutations

1. Evaluate without a calculator: $\frac{10!}{8!2!}$

2. A password consists of 4 different letters chosen from the word "COMPUTER".
a) How many arrangements of the letters in "COMPUTER" are there in total?
b) How many arrangements begin with a vowel?

3. Find the number of different ways the letters of the word "INDONESIA" can be arranged.

4. 5 boys and 4 girls sit in a row. How many arrangements are there if all the girls must sit together?

Worksheet 3 & 4: Combinations and Mixed Problems

1. Evaluate ${}^{12}C_4$.

2. A committee of 5 is to be selected from a group of 7 men and 6 women.
a) How many different committees can be formed?
b) How many committees consist of exactly 3 men and 2 women?
c) How many committees contain at least 1 woman?

3. A quiz team of 4 students is chosen from 10 students. The team is then arranged in a line for a photograph. How many different photographs can be taken?

💯 Chapter 5 Evaluation (Used in Meeting 6)

Instructions: Answer all questions. Show all working steps clearly. Total: 35 Marks. (Cambridge AS Level Style)

Part A: Basic Concepts & Factorials (10 Marks)

1. Simplify the algebraic expression completely: $\frac{(n+1)!}{(n-1)!}$. (3 marks)

2. Calculate the number of ways 6 different books can be arranged on a shelf. (2 marks)

3. State whether the following scenario requires a Permutation or a Combination, and explain why briefly: "Choosing a President, Vice President, and Treasurer from 15 candidates." (2 marks)

4. Calculate ${}^8P_3$ and ${}^8C_3$. (3 marks)

Part B: Permutations with Restrictions (12 Marks)

5. Find the number of different arrangements of the letters in the word "STATISTICS". (3 marks)

6. From a group of 8 people, a row of 5 people is to be seated.
a) How many possible seating arrangements are there? (2 marks)
b) Two specific people in the group, Andi and Budi, refuse to sit next to each other. How many arrangements of 5 people can be formed that INCLUDE both Andi and Budi, but they are NOT seated together? (7 marks)

Part C: Combinations & Complex Problem Solving (13 Marks)

7. A school debate team of 4 members is to be selected from a class of 9 girls and 7 boys.
a) Find the total number of possible teams that can be formed. (2 marks)
b) Find the number of teams that contain exactly 2 girls and 2 boys. (3 marks)
c) Find the number of teams that contain a majority of girls (more girls than boys). (4 marks)

8. Modeling Question: A security code consists of 2 letters chosen from 'A,B,C,D,E' followed by 3 digits chosen from '1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9'. Letters and digits cannot be repeated.
How many different codes can be generated if the letters must be in alphabetical order? (4 marks)
